The whitepaper in which I outlined the initial conception of ProjSpec was largely written in 1998, then revised in 2000.  Now, to me, it has the feel of being dated.   Yes, it accurately anticipated the importance of XML and of Web apps, but it necessarily had to await the emergence of some new technologies and improvement in my understanding of others.

 

Technologies have emerged (or just have become apparent to me) which begin to complete the description of what we have come to call S/A/F (Smart Adaptive Framework).  It is a less geeky name and one more descriptive of its intent.

The need for such a product still is apparent.  In the intervening years, every time I have talked with an executive, a manager, or a project director, I have asked this question: “What framework or environment do you use to manage your project(s)?  What app is there for coordinating the

 

 

details, for directing the production, for producing management information, and for providing executive summaries?”  Now granted, those individuals don’t constitute a statistically valid sample.  Nevertheless, not one has had much of an answer – not one!  They all agreed that they could use such a product, and they admitted that their projects were still generally run on (in my words) “stamina & good intentions”.

XML still remains at the heart of what I call the object database.  Capturing everything as XML objects is simple and fast, and it can now rely on the underlying database to store and retrieve them magically sight unseen.

 

Every piece of information (textual, numeric, and graphical) is captured as an object.  Objects coalesce into larger objects, and larger objects form into classes of objects.  Attributes can be tossed onto an object (in a free form manner) and be expected to stick.  Designers and users of the system do not need to have any a priori knowledge of what attributes will be appropriate for each object.

 

Search engines and clustering do the retrieval and categorizing of objects.  These technologies are mature now, produce accurate results in real time, and will only get better with each passing year.  With these engines integrated into S/A/F, end-users will view the clusters of objects based on any combination of attributes.  This leads to the description of the first product:

 

NoteObjects

 

NoteObjects, in its simplest form, provides a clustered tree-view of related information items, i.e. clustered XMLs.   A requirement for the NoteObjects app is that it has to be as easy to use as Notepad (or perhaps Excel – the fact is that MS Project is too complex and has too much baggage to be used by the average office worker).  

 

For NoteObjects, picture a version of Notepad in which every time you enter Carriage Return <CR> at the end of a line, that line of text is captured as an XML object.  Then with a fast clustering engine as the basis, users can view clusters of information via a tree control. 

 

Example:  as a manager, information pops into my head constantly, all day long, which I must remember to convey to my staff members.  Rather than a paper note, rather than an email, rather than a verbal instruction, rather than a voicemail, rather than waiting for a meeting, it is much, much easier to just type in the random thoughts.  NoteObjects would, behind the scenes, immediately capture the notes as XML objects, and the objects clustered together in any of several ways, based on the contents of each note. 

 

When I come into work in the morning, I’d use NoteObjects to display all notes which contain my name in order to see who may be asking me to do something or what information someone may be asking of me.  Then I might display all the notes with are related to one particular project in order to see what kind of activities have been taking place on that project.  I might display all items which have the words “done”, “finished”, or “completed”.   (Note:  those would be clustered and presented to me, not a keyword search by me nor having to know how to invoke some stored SQL.)   I might display on percent done.  I might display on any of dozens and dozens of attributes.  The significant point is that no one had to a priori define what these attributes are.  Search engines and spell checkers now search on “sounds like”, “near miss”, “means a similar thing”, etc.  Thus users are allowed to throw attributes into the mix in the manner that their business labels things.  A similar significant point is that the attributes are in the terminology of the company’s culture and business model, not in computer-geek-speak which historically database applications have imposed on the users.

 

Visualization Engine

 

There has to be an intuitive way to graphically see the objects in relation to each other and to have the objects colored based on the type of attribute and it current values.  There has to be an intuitive way to watch the objects grow or wane (become more important or less important), glow or dim (based on the magnitude of attribute values), change shape (as the information about the object is relatively complete, relatively incomplete, or skewed in one direction or the other). 

 

The graphics must be 3-dimensional.  It is actually a multi-dimensional problem, but we humans see things in three dimensions.  So there has to be a way to “walk through the model”, i.e. see different dimensions from different angles, to look downward to get a high-level macro view of the project, to take a ground level view of the relative position of objects around you and look up from beneath to get an under-surface view of the model infrastructure.

 

The connections between objects are extremely important, as significant as the objects themselves.   Each connection between any two objects must have attributes of its own, i.e. the direction of the connection, whether it is bi-directional, strength or tenuousness of the connection, time and expense related to the flow of information between objects.  The line style (solid, dashed, dotted, etc.) and thickness of the line are determined by the strength and nature of the connection.

 

The product is a tool for visualizing the essence of complex projects, a model that “forms itself up”.  To use it would be intuitive and easy, with a mode for “walking through” the model.  The reality is that every project becomes exceedingly complex even within hours, certainly within a few days – such a capability would be invaluable.

 

That complexity inevitably surpasses the ability of individuals to keep track of all the details and follow the intricate web of connections.  Therefore flexible visualization apps are needed to expand this capability for the user.  Two technologies exist to assist in this effort – integrated with the graphics they become analysis tools, i.e. products of S/A/F –  XTM and FCM – XML Topic Maps and Fuzzy Cognitive Maps.  Integrated with network routing algorithms, they’ll show logical paths through the connections, giving the user the ability to see the connections that have been made, or should be made, are significant, or are insignificant, are taking on more significance all the time, or are diminishing in importance.

 

A “philosophy” of S/A/F could be that every object be considered in its own right as a Web Service.  In this concept, every object “announces” to the overall system:  “here I am”, “this is what I do”, “this is what I need”, “who wants what I offer?”, “who has what I need?”  Objects “negotiate” with each other, “interact” with each other, form connections with each other, etc.  In this manner objects grow or wane as do organisms in other complex systems.  When a significant thing happens to an object or when a significant connection is made, then the system sends messages and flashes signals, so users’ attention is drawn to the area where they should be paying attention.

 

Business Outputs

 

This is the central product delivered by S/A/F – business outputs to every business function across the enterprise.  We are in the business of process modeling, project modeling, decision modeling, business modeling, and the output from the system must focus on business products – summaries, totals, status, budgets, time lines, resource requirements, progress, logjams, revenue tracking, notification of what is working, notification of what is missing – the business products needed by business decision makers to keep their projects on track and get their products to market ahead of their competitors.

 

The system has to start producing those products from the outset.  Note the green, blue, red shading in the diagram – useful products from the inception, expanded projects in the mid-term, advanced products in the long term.

 

The framework, just by collecting, clustering, and classifying information, is enough to take most employees and their projects over-the-top – increasing success, productivity, profits.  In and of itself, that is sufficient to justify development, distribution, and adoption by the market.

 

The exponential payback is when decision engines go beyond just organizing data and effective visualization.  When decision engines undergird growing, evolving, adaptive models is when project members can expect to be pointed to critical points and emerging problems (in a relatively unstable, still emerging model) and to efficiencies and high payoff points (in a mature, relatively stable, but always evolving model). 

 

What will fuel this decision engine?  Fuzzy logic?  Bayesian logic?  Neural processing?   I expect a hybrid of these, combined with other advances with which at this point I am just not aware.  This is not an area in which I profess any great expertise, but what is certain is that just over the horizon there is going to be sufficient processing power to handle it.

 

How expansive should a claim of market potential be for this product?  With a product as easy to use as Notepad, visualization that is intuitive for everyone to see and use, business outputs and guidance for every business unit in the enterprise, tight integration with MS Office suite, models built on robust, adaptive, believable decision engines, all in the terminology homegrown in each business … there is no reason not to believe that the potential is for it to be used in every office in every business worldwide, all of which can benefit from:
