The task of the EISYS team was to conduct a Needs Assessment for a municipal GIS, which was being put in place to manage extremely rapid growth in the city of Kuala Kencana on the remote island of Irian Jaya in eastern Indonesia. The challenge was to quickly assess the system requirements for multiple departments, multiple levels within each department, and multiple applications at each level. Utility Billing System

Kuala Kencana, Irian Jaya, Indonesia

1997-1998

From concept to delivery in 6 months! While we originally evaluated several commercially marketed billing systems for municipal utilities, the challenge of delivering a UBS to Kuala Kencana was for it to be customizable for the unique aspects of utilities in a new town in a remote area and for it to be maintainable on-site in Irian Jaya by the local IS. So instead we developed and delivered a straightforward Visual Basic application system which automated the administrative practices of the KK utilities.

In this case, a workable spec had to balance "high" tech with "effective" tech. The system we delivered met all the functionality for accounting and billing for the city utilities, but perhaps the most significant technical aspect was that the software system was completely self-contained. By that I mean that it was implemented in standard Visual Basic on Windows 95, using no third-party objects or libraries. Even the Help system was developed using built-in Win95 capabilities. The significance of this rigorous adherence to "standard" development is that the KK Town Administrator can maintain the system locally, can recruit programming talent from the pool of advanced programmers "in-country", is not dependent on vendor support from half-way around the world, and is not hindered by proprietary components which likely could not have been maintained by their distant developers in this remote site.

The UBS code was implemented in an entirely "resource-independent" manner. So although original implementation in English may have been dictated by the tight time frame and the lack of Indonesian language skills on the part of the programmer (me!) and the proficiency and comfort with English that the Kuala Kencana town staff has, the UBS system could be translated into a localized Bahasa Indonesia version quickly and simply by translating the resource files and help documents.

ESRI-ERDAS Live-Link

ESRI Redlands, CA - ERDAS Atlanta, GA

1987-1989

ESRI is the leader in mapping and vector-based GIS. ERDAS is the leader in image processing and raster-based GIS. The ESRI-ERDAS Live Link was the first commercially available product to geographically register and display a vector map over an image backdrop such as a satellite image or aerial photo.

All complex projects are the result of collaborative effort by many talented individuals. In the case of the Live Link product, without exaggeration or boasting, I'll take sole credit for conceiving, proposing, designing, specing, and developing this capability. A dozen years later, when such maps are omnipresent on the web, on portable PCs, and in rental cars, it is not often that such a capability can be traced to a single developer.

There was a great deal more to this product than just registering a map to an image. Both software packages, Arc/Info and ERDAS ran in full capacity in their native environments. They shared geographic information in runtime through a shared-memory area. Thus they notified each other of commands and options the user had selected, so the other package could automatically respond with an appropriate action. Remember that this was all before the 90's event-driven and client-server programming paradigms. In fact, it was even made to work on 640K DOS, as well as on various flavors of Unix, VMS, and for God's sake even PRIMOS.

Radar Modeling

Pt. Mugu Naval Air Station - Port Hueneme, CA

1982-1983

When Ronald Reagan was President, he flew on Air Force One into and out of the Pt. Mugu Naval Air Station. Because of the high surrounding mountains, there was a blind spot in the FAA's In-Route Air Traffic Control system. GTRI was contracted to produce a radar model for the base and to recommend an optimum site for a new radar placement that would eliminate the blind spot.

I was the lead programmer on the project. We obtained DEMs (Digital Elevation Model) from the U. S. Geological Survey and DTED data (Digital Terrain Elevation Data) from the Defense Mapping Agency. We calculated line of sight for a 360 degree radar sweep to the peak elevations, i.e. the ridge line surrounding the base.

We also produced a geographic map of the base and a three-dimensional CAD model of all the NAS buildings, fences, towers, etc., i.e any structure that would reflect a radar beam. We contracted with the GTRI Radar Lab for a radar model to measure radar reflection and false targets. Using the elevation data, the geographic map, the CAD model, and the radar reflection model, we identified two on-base sites which eliminated the blind spots and minimized radar reflection.

 

Device Independent 2-D & 3-D Graphics Package

IBM, Rochester, MN

1982

This project allowed IBM to market graphics capabilities for one of its early business-grade dot-matrix printers. Waterborne Intruder Detection System

Naval Coastal Systems Center - Panama City, FL

1979-1982

WIDS was a major GTRI research project to adapt radar technology for tracking surface vessels near shorelines and through ship channels. WIDS was a multi-processor system which integrated real-time signal processing and pattern detection on custom microprocessors with output to early vector graphics displays. Three bodies of software - microprocessor assembler, structured Fortran app, and high-level graphics - communicated in real-time.

For all the leading-edge technologies that were part of this project, the most significant innovation was the programming standards that we evolved in order to manage the multi-level complexity of the software and hardware. Between phases #2 and #3 of the project, we streamlined, standardized, and documented the code to a level where by phases #3 and #4 we observed orders of magnitude improvement in productivity. Changes could literally be made in an afternoon that had taken programmers two to four weeks back in phases #1 and #2. This is the project where I learned firsthand what makes the difference between hacker code and professional software.

 

Field Exploitation of Elevation Data

U.S. Army Topographic Laboratory - Ft. Belvoir, VA

1981-1984

The FEED project was a joint research effort between GTRI and the U.S. Army's Topographic Laboratory to demonstrate how digital elevation data could be applied in operational units in the field. A mini-computer system was developed for a mobile van, and digital terrain databases were prepared for several locales where exercises are regularly conducted. The FEED van was on demonstration or participated in exercises at Ft. Belvoir, VA, Ft. Bragg, NC, Ft. Huachuca, AZ, and HdqtrsCINCPACCOM, Honolulu, HI .

Planimeter Application

Durham City Planning Department - Durham, NC

1975-1977

While I was a graduate student at the University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill, I worked as a programmer in the Durham City Planning Department. The planning director had become so frustrated by unfulfilled requests of the city's mainframe IT/Data department that he decided to purchase a mini-computer for his department and hire planning students to program it. That was a revolutionary idea at that time, and when he used departmental funds to procure a DEC PDP-11, it was literally the only one anywhere between Washington, D.C. and Atlanta .

I programmed an automated map planimeter program, with which the planning technicians could make map measurements and area calculations on maps of various scales using a tablet digitizer. I also wrote programs to plot the Census DIME maps of the Durham metropolitan area and to report summary census tract demographic statistics.

For all the attention and praise that these graphics applications invariably got, the most significant programming lesson I learned was so very fundamental. It was that in-line technical documentation of source code is what differentiates software from hacker code to professional quality software. In the second year, I was asked to revise and enhance the planimeter code which I had written in the first year. When I looked at my own code, I could not make any sense of my own logic and had to painfully retrace the program step by step to re-learn it. Thus, I learned that documentation is not for the next guy, but it is for yourself, because six months later, you will not be proficient on your own code without it. Phase II of the planimeter program was completed with thorough documentation and that is a programming standard that I have taken with me to all subsequent projects through my career.
