This project produced a prototype field data collection unit for use by exploration geologists in their field activities around the world. The software was developed as an ArcView (ESRI) Extension, and it integrated components from numerous technologies - GPS, satellite imagery, digital photographs, voice capture, geographic basemaps, geologic maps, and input forms for field geology input. It was packaged on a commercially available, Pentium-class, Windows 95, pen-based computer.

I was the technical lead on this project and designed and spec'ed the modules and components in collaboration with ARCO geologists and engineers. EISYS Ft.Collins programmer Randy How performed all of the integration programming in a remarkable six weeks, plus Randy brought the expertise to the project for programming the ArcView extension. Randy & I programmed together almost daily, he in Colorado and me in Oregon, via web-based, long-distance collaboration technologies. We viewed screens simultaneously, edited common documents, shared files, compiled programs, and even took control of each other's mouse on occasion to demonstrate various points and techniques.

 

The potential enterprise impact of such a product goes far beyond the increased efficiencies in the field work itself. Doing field mapping, taking digital photos, and capturing the geologist's voice description of what is observed, all in a standard ArcView project format means that the field work can be transmitted back from the field to headquarters via email or web app. Rather than the historical weeks delay between a geologist's field work and the posting of the data back in the lab, the maps, photos, and verbal description can be available to all of the company's far-flung engineers and scientists within hours.
