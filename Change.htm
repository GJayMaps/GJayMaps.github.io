It's All About Change

An Artist in Residence Project

In anticipation of the move to the new Linus Pauling Middle School, the "It's All About Change" banners were created by Carol Nelson's math classes at Westland Middle School in the spring of 2004.  Kerry proposed the design for the project and led the students in making the blocks for the banners.

Why art in math class?  During the project, students got a real world taste of how math applies to everyday life, especially art.  They did the math necessary to calculate how large the banners should be, and how large each collage block should be, keeping in mind that 230 students would each be making one block.  They extrapolated from architect's drawings how high the wall would be, determined from class experiments how high the average middle school student could jump, and deduced how high the banners should hang.   They calculated the yardage and supplies necessary for the project, and estimated the cost of the project. 

The theme of change reflects the changes that the students have found in their new school environment, and the changes that they will see in their environment and in themselves as they grow up.  The theme is visually represented by the four ancient elements of Earth, Wind, Fire, and Water, and each student interpreted their element in a fabric collage block.  Many students also learned to use an iron for the first time as they fused their fabric shapes to a heat-sensitive backing.  The collages were covered by a layer of sheer nylon tulle netting, and stitched into place by Ms. McFall.  

A traditional quilt pattern sometimes known as "birds in flight" was used as a base to construct the banner, featuring blocks with triangles arranged much like the V-shaped configurations we see in the Willamette Valley sky when migrating geese fly overhead.   Not coincidentally, the triangle is the letter of the Greek alphabet "delta", which means change or difference in mathematical symbolism.

The words on the banner represent a translation of the element's name into the languages spoken by students in this school.  The translations were provided by the English as a Second Language class, and represent some of the 16 languages spoken here.

 But what about Duct Tape?  Alert students will notice that the Duct Tape banner (made to illustrate Mr. Stephen's thesis that duct tape is the missing Fifth Ancient Element) is not hanging with the other four.  At this writing, it's not sure where this banner will find its home here at Linus Pauling, but the science rooms are a good bet!
